There's a moment in most design critiques where someone says *"I know what I'm trying to say, I just can't figure out how to say it."* The room nods. The problem isn't the idea — it's the translation from intent to artifact.

Prompting has the same structure. The gap between what you mean and what you write is the design problem. And like most design problems, it rewards iteration more than perfection on the first try.

## The brief analogy

A prompt is a brief. A bad brief produces bad work not because the designer is bad but because the constraints were wrong, the goal was unclear, or the tone wasn't set. The same thing happens with models.

When I started treating prompts as briefs — with context, constraints, tone, and an explicit success criterion — the outputs got dramatically more useful. Not because the model changed, but because I stopped assuming it already knew what I wanted.

## What a good brief includes

- **Context:** Why does this exist? What problem does it solve?
- **Constraints:** What can't it be? What's out of scope?
- **Tone:** What does good feel like? Give an example if you have one.
- **Success criterion:** How will you know when it's right?

Most prompts have one of these at most. Usually zero.

## The memory problem

The thing that breaks the brief analogy is memory. A collaborator accumulates context over time — they know your preferences, your history, your weird opinions about type size. The model starts fresh every time.

This means the brief has to carry more weight than it would with a human. All the implicit context that a colleague would just *know* has to be made explicit. It's more work upfront and less work everywhere else.

> The model has read everything and remembers nothing. Write accordingly.

## What I've changed

I keep a running document of prompt patterns that work — not specific prompts, but structural patterns. *"Before doing X, first establish Y"* is one. *"Show me two approaches before committing"* is another. They're not rules, they're orientations.

The practice of prompting, like the practice of writing briefs, is the practice of learning what you actually want.
