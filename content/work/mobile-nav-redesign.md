## The tab bar problem

Nine tabs. Four of them used by fewer than 8% of users. The two highest-traffic destinations required two taps each to reach from the home screen. The information architecture had grown organically over three years and nobody had looked at it as a whole.

## Research

Six days of diary studies, 40 participants. The finding that changed everything: users weren't confused by the navigation — they had learned to ignore it entirely and use search for everything. The nav had become so unreliable as a wayfinding tool that people had built a workaround.

## The redesign

The new model has three elements:

- **A persistent home action** — the one thing every user does every session, always one tap away
- **A contextual tray** — surfaces the 3 most relevant secondary actions based on what you're doing
- **A full menu** — for everything else, accessed deliberately

The contextual tray was the bet. It meant the system needed to know what "relevant" meant per context. We defined six context states and the action set for each.

> The best navigation is the one you don't notice because you're always already where you need to be.

## Outcome

Average tap depth to core tasks: 3.2 → 1.4. Search usage for primary navigation dropped 61%. Support tickets related to "can't find X" down 44% in 60 days post-launch.
